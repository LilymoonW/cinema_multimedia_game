# Shadow Quest

Try playing here -> https://lilysun.itch.io/shadow-quest

> *"A hero's journey to eradicate the shadows."*

![Title screen](screenshots/title.png)

My cinema class project is a short RPG that uses cute fantasy aesthetics to disguise a darker allegory about propaganda, scapegoating, and violence. Through branching endings and increasingly uncomfortable encounters, the game asks players to question the acts of violence under the guise of glory .

## The Setup

![boss](assets/sprites/boss.png)
![boss alternative form](assets/sprites/boss1.png)
![walk](assets/sprites/walk.png)

You play as a small, cute-looking character woken up by Pip, a tiny sparkling spirit who tells you the world is in danger and that you've been chosen to cleanse it. Pip is friendly, encouraging, and constantly reminds you that the "Shadows" are corrupt and dangerous.

The intro sets the tone exactly like the kinds of games you've seen before:

> *"Hi, I've been waiting for your appearance."*  
> *"The world is in danger of corrupt individuals."*  
> *"Hold the right arrow key to walk and purify enemies!"*

That is the story the game hands you.

The rest of the game is about whether you believe it.

![Walking through the world](screenshots/walking.png)

You walk to the right through a soft pastel world filled with clouds, trees, and glowing colors. Ahead of you are small dark figures called the Shadows.

## Gameplay

The game is structured around four encounters:

**1 shadow → 2 shadows → 4 shadows → 1 boss**

Combat is a small turn-based RPG battle system inspired by classic menu-based games.

Each round:
1. Pip chooses to *Cheer* or *Heal*
2. You choose to *Fight* or *Run*
3. If you fight, you cast either *Shine* or *Purify*
4. The shadows attack back

Every enemy defeated gives you **+1 HOPE**.

![Battle screen](screenshots/battle.png)

After each fight, the game asks:

> *"...will you look at what's left of them?"*

You can either:
- **Observe**
- **Walk away**

Observing reveals more about what the Shadows really are.

## What the Story Is Actually About

The shadows die very easily. A little too easily.

As the game progresses, cracks begin to appear in Pip's narration:

> *"...mother..."*  
> *"...we were just running..."*  
> *"...the light hurts..."*

If you choose to Observe after battles, it becomes clear the Shadows are not monsters at all, but frightened and displaced people being labeled as dangerous.

Pip constantly interrupts, redirects, and insists they deserve it.

The game slowly shifts from a cute fantasy RPG into an allegory about propaganda, scapegoating, and how easily violence can be framed as righteousness.

---

## The Allegory

Underneath the fantasy setup, the game is an allegory about scapegoating.

More specifically, it explores the way famine, disease, and hardship are often blamed on outsiders, refugees, or vulnerable groups, and how propaganda convinces ordinary people to participate in violence against them.

The boss letter reveals the truth:

> *"They slowly turned against us, blaming us for famine and disease."*

The "Shadows" are not monsters. They are people who were displaced, starving, and hunted after being labeled dangerous.

Pip is intentionally designed to be the least suspicious figure in the game.

Bright. Cute. Sparkling. The "light."

The thing calling itself the light is also the thing telling you not to look too closely.

## Why I Made This

In class, we were talking about parables and allegories, and one of our exercises was writing one through a creative medium. I thought games were an especially interesting format because they can make players participate in ideas instead of just watching them happen.

I wanted to make a game that slowly subverts the player's expectations and makes them increasingly uncomfortable with actions that the genre usually rewards without question.

The project was inspired by games like Omori and Undertale, along with the tonal shifts you get in Black Mirror episodes.

I actually do not play many games myself. I mostly watch streams and video essays, which honestly might be why it was easier for me to notice the structure behind these mechanics.

The project itself is short and simple, but I wanted the mechanics and message to feel directly connected.
